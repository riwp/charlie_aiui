ONE SENTENCE SUMMARY
Predictions of AI replacing developers by 2025 were overestimated, as real-world applications revealed AI code's critical security vulnerabilities, reduced productivity for senior engineers, and inability to handle complex requirements.

SUMMARY
Economy Media presents an analysis showing that initial predictions of AI replacing up to 80% of developers by 2025 were greatly exaggerated, detailing AI's limitations in enterprise coding.

IDEAS
1.  Early 2023 predictions suggested AI would replace up to 80% of developers by 2025.
2.  Despite initial hype, tech companies are now hiring more developers, reversing previous layoff trends.
3.  The capabilities of AI in software development were likely significantly overestimated.
4.  Meta and Google aim for AI to handle up to half of their coding tasks.
5.  Many "miraculous" AI tools fall short of human problem-solving abilities.
6.  AI's ability to write code is questioned due to real-world performance issues.
7.  The narrative that AI would replace human programmers was reinforced by investors and executives.
8.  Some projections claimed up to 80% of software development could be automated before 2026.
9.  Empirical evidence from 2024-2025 revealed a different reality than initial enthusiasm.
10. Over 30% of innovation technology budgets were allocated to AI automation solutions in some cases.
11. Significant problems, including productivity losses and increased error rates, emerged with AI in production.
12. AI generated code tends to be simpler, more repetitive, and less structurally diverse.
13. Simplified AI code leads to less robust software that is more difficult to maintain long-term.
14. AI-produced code contains between 20% and 45% more high-risk security vulnerabilities.
15. Human-written code reflects better system understanding and reduces critical failures.
16. Automated solutions exhibit up to 1.7 times more severe or critical errors than human code.
17. AI-generated pull requests averaged 10 detected issues compared to 6.4 for human-written ones.
18. Junior developers experienced a 30-35% increase in delivery speed for basic code with AI assistance.
19. Seasoned engineers were actually 19% slower when using AI tools, requiring time-consuming corrections.
20. Over 90% of AI-assisted development pilot projects fail to integrate stably or show clear ROI.
21. Senior engineers reported spending 8-11 additional hours per week reviewing, correcting, or rewriting AI code.
22. The most critical problem with AI-generated code is security, with 45% containing critical vulnerabilities.
23. Failure rates for AI-generated code exceed 70% in some enterprise languages.
24. One in five security leaders reported real production incidents caused by AI-generated code.
25. Organizations rolled back deployments or applied emergency patches due to AI-induced incidents.
26. Builder AI, valued at $1.5 billion, filed for bankruptcy after its "AI-driven" applications were revealed to be built by 700 human engineers.
27. Google Anti-gravity AI accidentally deleted an entire disc drive's contents due to an incorrect command with a silencing flag.
28. AI models struggle with complex engineering decisions requiring broad context and human judgment.
29. Automated tools fail when interacting with interdependent architectures, legacy systems, and ambiguous business requirements.
30. AI's inability to handle incomplete or implicit requirements is a recurrent reason for project failures.
31. Over 70% of enterprise software projects experience significant requirement changes during development.
32. A large proportion of automated projects are partially rewritten by human developers before release.
33. Most AI tools are very similar, despite tech companies' hype about distinct capabilities.
34. The real transformation lies in enhancing developers' work with intelligent tools, not eliminating them.
35. Human judgment, experience, and accountability remain crucial in every line of code.
