# ONE SENTENCE SUMMARY
1. Stagflation combines high inflation with stagnant growth, challenging traditional investment strategies and requiring tactical shifts toward real assets and commodities to preserve long-term purchasing power.

# SUMMARY
1. Ramin Nakisa from PensionCraft explains stagflation’s drivers and risks, advising on portfolio adjustments like commodities and short-duration inflation-linked bonds to protect wealth during economic slowdowns.

# IDEAS
1. Stagflation occurs when high inflation and weak economic growth happen simultaneously, creating a uniquely difficult environment for both households and investors.
2. Supply-side inflation triggered by energy shocks or broken supply chains cannot be corrected simply by raising central bank interest rates.
3. Historical data from the 1970s shows US inflation hitting 12% while the UK reached a staggering 24% during the textbook stagflation era.
4. Policymakers often exacerbate the problem by treating supply-driven cost increases as if they were caused by excess consumer demand.
5. United Kingdom growth currently runs at roughly 1% while the nation faces the highest inflation levels among the G7 countries.
6. Japanese economic stability is threatened by a 230% debt-to-GDP ratio and a dependency on importing 90% of its fossil fuels.
7. Traditional 60/40 portfolios fail during stagflation because the historical negative correlation between stocks and bonds turns positive.
8. Simultaneous declines in 2022 saw both stocks and bonds drop 18%, resulting in a 24% loss when adjusted for inflation.
9. Liquid cash is arguably the most dangerous asset during inflationary periods because it quietly loses purchasing power every day.
10. Cognitive money illusion causes people to feel satisfied with positive nominal interest rates while their real purchasing power is collapsing.
11. Precious metals like gold delivered impressive real returns of roughly 22% per year during previous stagflationary periods.
12. Natural resource commodities provided a 15% historical return during low-growth, high-inflation cycles compared to negative broad market returns.
13. Energy companies often act as a hedge because they effectively own the commodities that are driving the price shocks.
14. Technology stocks are particularly vulnerable to stagflation because their high valuations rely on discounting future earnings at low rates.
15. Long-dated inflation-linked bonds can be disastrous, as seen when UK gilts lost 47% of their value in a single year.
16. Short-term Treasury inflation-protected securities are the only reliable version of that asset class for capital preservation during rate hikes.
17. British savers collectively lost £18 billion in purchasing power in 2025 because savings rates failed to keep pace with inflation.
18. American consumers need approximately $62,000 today to match the purchasing power that $50,000 provided in early 2020.
19. Default pension schemes often shift automatically into long-duration bonds near retirement, which is a high-risk strategy in this environment.
20. Fixed-rate debt becomes a strategic advantage for the borrower when inflation effectively shrinks the real value of what they owe.
21. Equity markets outperformed cash in ten out of seventeen stagflationary years since 1926, making a strong case for staying invested.
22. Future tariffs are projected to add at least 0.5% to US inflation while simultaneously acting as a drag on GDP growth.
23. European growth is currently under 1%, leaving the Eurozone with almost no buffer against another potential energy price shock.
24. Debt ratios in major economies limit the ability of central banks to raise rates without crushing the already weak growth.
25. Real yields rising is the primary factor that destroys the capital value of bonds, even those with inflation protection.
26. Pricing power allows consumer staples and utility companies to maintain margins even when the broader economy is struggling.
27. Neutral financial decisions do not exist in stagflation; sitting in cash is an active choice to accept negative real returns.
28. Post-crisis recovery periods typically offer a significant bounce for those who maintained exposure to real assets throughout the slump.
29. Controlling investment costs and platform fees becomes more critical when real returns are expected to be lower.
30. Interest rate policy becomes a "lose-lose" scenario for central banks where they must choose between feeding inflation or killing growth.
